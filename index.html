Colonial Beginnings

Est. 1710

New Bern was first settled in 1710 by Swiss and German immigrants, who named the settlement after their former home of Bern, Switzerland.

North Carolina

In 1729, North Carolina became an English royal colony, but it lacked a permanent government capitol.

▶▶

1767

But if we fast forward to 1767,

North Carolina has a new Royal Governor named William Tryon, and he chooses New Bern as the colony's new capitol,

New Bern = govt.

and to solidify New Bern as the permanent government seat, Governor Tryon commissioned

NEW

(also expensive)

a new governor's mansion and capitol office in New Bern -- at the expense of NC colonists.

Unhappy taxpayers referred to the extravagent building as Tryon "Palace" -- and the name stuck.

By 1771, when a new Royal Governor named Josiah Martin moved into the colonial capitol,

colonists were becoming more and more unsatisfied with the Royal Government and taxation.

1775

In May 1775, facing increasing pressure and threat of capture from American Patriots,

Governor Martin = gone

The First State Capitol

1783-1794

And With the end of the war came the beginning of North Carolina's independent statehood, and New Bern's distinguishment as the first state capitol.

Governor Alexander Martin was the first of the North Carolina State Governors to live in Tryon Palace,

and in 1791, he played host to Pres. George Washington, who attended a ball at the governor's mansion.

1794

In 1794, after serving

> Twenty Years

more than 20 years as the colonial and state capitol

Tryon Palace was retired from government work,

and the new state capitol was established in the more centrally-located city of Raleigh.

Tryon Palace was temporarily repurposed as a lodging house and school

1798

until 1798, when the main building burned down from a fire in the cellar.

Tryon Palace

Even without Tryon Palace, New Bern still found itself involved in political matters.

During the Civil War, New Bern fell to Union forces at the Battle of New Bern, and the city remained under Union occupation until the war ended in 1865.

Many of the deserted homes in New Bern became occupied by Union soldiers and used as bunk houses or hospitals. Even Tryon Palace's last-remaining stable office was repurposed as chapel and school following the war.

160+ years later...

houses and businesses and highways, oh my!

160 years after the Palace's destruction, houses, businesses, and even a highway were built over its remains.

But beginning in the 1930s, a group of New Bern citizens began campaigning for reconstruction of the Palace estate. They wanted to pay tribute to North Carolina's first capitol and its forgotten Palace.

Video about reconstruction

Reconstruction was completed in 1959,

and Tryon Palace reopened its gates to the public.

Today, Tryon Palace draws a lot of tourism to New Bern

around 180,000 visitors annually.

And the history surrounding North Carolina's first capitol is kept alive by the Palace estate

and a number of costumed volunteers.

Tryon Palace has even received some publicity help from Hollywood. The TV show "Sleepy Hollow" has used the Palace and other New Bern historic houses as sets for a few episodes.
